Multiple Choice

PreBot.C (early), rVRG and DRG (late)
PRG and DRG only

Correct! The PreBot.C fires during early inspiration, then the rVRG and DRG fire during late inspiration — together producing the graded ramp signal that excites the phrenic nerve.
Review Slide 11. The PreBot.C initiates early inspiration, and the rVRG + DRG contribute during late inspiration. Together they produce the graded ramp signal.
Clinical Thinking
Why is it physiologically advantageous to have a ramped (gradually increasing) signal rather than an all-or-nothing burst to the phrenic nerve?
The ramp signal produces a graded, steady increase in lung volume during inspiration — this creates eupnea (normal, smooth breathing). An all-or-nothing burst would produce sudden, sharp inspiratory gasps, which would be inefficient for gas exchange and uncomfortable.

Clinical Scenario
A patient presents with prolonged, gasping inspirations separated by brief expirations. Imaging reveals a pontine lesion. Which abnormal respiratory pattern is this, and what structure is most likely affected?
This is apneusis — prolonged inspirations occur because the "off switch" for inspiration is damaged. A pontine lesion affecting the PRG (pneumotaxic center) and/or the Bötzinger Complex removes the inhibitory signals that normally terminate the inspiratory ramp. Without this brake, inspiration continues unchecked.

Multiple Choice
Central chemoreceptors respond primarily to hypercapnia rather than acidosis in arterial blood. Why?
H⁺ ions are rapidly metabolized in the CNS
H⁺ ions cannot readily cross the BBB, but CO₂ can — once across, CO₂ is converted to H⁺
Central chemoreceptors lack H⁺ receptors
CO₂ directly binds to central chemoreceptor proteins
Correct! This is the key mechanism: H⁺ is charged and can't easily cross the BBB. CO₂ freely crosses, then is converted to H⁺ via the carbonic acid reaction (CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻). So CO₂ has a potent indirect effect through H⁺ generation in the CNS.
The key is the Blood-Brain Barrier: H⁺ ions (charged) can't cross the BBB easily. CO₂ (uncharged) crosses freely, then gets converted to H⁺ via carbonic acid. So CO₂ stimulates central chemoreceptors indirectly through local H⁺ production. Review Slides 23–24.
Clinical Thinking
Peripheral chemoreceptors receive 40× the blood flow per gram of tissue compared to the brain. Why is this necessary for their function?
The massive blood flow ensures that the tissue extracts virtually 0% of the O₂ passing through, so the chemoreceptors are always bathed in arterial blood at normal PO₂ (~100 mmHg). If blood flow were lower, local O₂ consumption would drop the PO₂ around the sensors — they'd essentially be seeing venous blood and would falsely signal hypoxia even when arterial oxygenation is normal.

CO₂ Response Curve, Desensitization & COPD
▼
⏸️
Pause after Slide 27. Slides 26–27 are a two-part sequence: the CO₂ response curve (pharmacology crossover) and the COPD desensitization mechanism (classic board question). Understand both cold.
Multiple Choice
On the CO₂ ventilatory response curve (Slide 26), the slope of each line represents chemoreceptor sensitivity. Which condition produces the STEEPEST slope (greatest sensitivity)?
Normal awake individual at normal PO₂
Hypoxia combined with hypercapnia
Normal individual during sleep
Patient under general anesthesia
Correct! When hypoxia and hypercapnia occur together, the peripheral and central chemoreceptors potentiate each other's response — producing the steepest slope. From steepest to flattest: hypoxia+hypercapnia → awake → sleep → drugs (opioids, barbiturates, anesthetics).
The steepest slope occurs when hypoxia and hypercapnia are combined — potentiation between peripheral and central chemoreceptors produces a massive ventilatory response. Remember the hierarchy from steepest to flattest: hypoxia+hypercapnia → awake → sleep → drugs. Review Slide 26.
Clinical Thinking
Why is mechanical ventilation mandatory during general anesthesia? Think about what the CO₂ response curve looks like for a patient under anesthetic agents.
Anesthetic agents dramatically flatten the CO₂ response curve. A patient under general anesthesia cannot adequately increase ventilation in response to rising PCO₂ — the brainstem's CO₂ alarm system is pharmacologically silenced. Without mechanical ventilation, PCO₂ would rise dangerously. This is the same mechanism by which opioid overdose kills through respiratory depression.
Multiple Choice
In chronic hypercapnia (e.g., a COPD patient), why does the central chemoreceptor response to elevated CO₂ diminish over time?
Central chemoreceptors physically degenerate
The BBB becomes impermeable to CO₂
Renal compensation raises blood [HCO₃⁻], which buffers excess CO₂ and normalizes CSF pH
Peripheral chemoreceptors take over completely
Correct! The kidneys compensate by retaining HCO₃⁻, which buffers the excess CO₂. This normalizes the pH around central chemoreceptors, blunting their response. The primary ventilatory drive then shifts to peripheral chemoreceptor stimulation by hypoxia.
The answer involves metabolic compensation: the kidneys increase blood [HCO₃⁻] over 1-2 days, which buffers the excess CO₂ and reduces the H⁺-mediated stimulus to central chemoreceptors. Review Slide 27.
Clinical Scenario
A COPD patient with chronic CO₂ retention relies on hypoxic drive for ventilation. A colleague suggests administering 100% supplemental O₂. Based on the lecture, why could this be dangerous, and what O₂ saturation target does recent evidence support?
High-flow O₂ corrects the patient's hypoxia — but in doing so, it eliminates the hypoxic stimulus to the peripheral chemoreceptors. Since the central chemoreceptors are already desensitized, the patient has now lost both ventilatory drives. Ventilation drops, CO₂ accumulates further, and the patient may become obtunded (CO₂ narcosis). Recent evidence suggests targeting an O₂ saturation of 88–92% — this corrects dangerous hypoxia while preserving enough hypoxic drive to maintain ventilation. The key is controlled, titrated oxygen — not high-flow 100% O₂.

Multiple Choice
Why is the initial increase in ventilation at the onset of exercise considered "anticipatory" rather than chemoreceptor-driven?
Peripheral chemoreceptors are inhibited by muscle activity
Blood pH drops before PO₂ changes
Chemical signals (↓PO₂, ↑PCO₂, ↓pH) are too slow to account for the immediate ventilatory increase
Central chemoreceptors are desensitized during exercise
Correct! The ventilatory increase at exercise onset is nearly instantaneous — far too fast for blood gas changes to have reached chemoreceptors. Exercise likely triggers direct stimulation of the respiratory center ("anticipatory respiration"), possibly as a learned response.
The key insight is timing: chemical signals take time to build up and reach chemoreceptors, but ventilation increases almost immediately when exercise begins. This means direct stimulation of the respiratory center — not chemoreceptor feedback — drives the initial response. Review Slide 29.

Sleep Apnea & Chemoreceptor Control
▼
⏸️
Pause after Slide 30. Sleep apnea is heavily tested on boards with connections to obesity, cardiovascular consequences, and pulmonary hypertension.
Multiple Choice
During an obstructive apneic episode in sleep, what happens to arterial PO₂ and PCO₂?
PO₂ ↑ and PCO₂ ↓
PO₂ ↓ and PCO₂ ↑
Both PO₂ and PCO₂ ↓
No change — the episode is too brief
Correct! Without ventilation, O₂ isn't replenished (PO₂ ↓) and CO₂ isn't eliminated (PCO₂ ↑). Both peripheral and central chemoreceptors detect these changes and send strong afferent signals to stimulate ventilation, which jerks the patient awake.
During apnea (no breathing), gas exchange stops: O₂ drops (↓PO₂) and CO₂ accumulates (↑PCO₂). This is sensed by both sets of chemoreceptors, triggering a strong ventilatory response that wakes the patient.
Multiple Choice
What is the primary mechanism of airway obstruction in obstructive sleep apnea?
Laryngospasm triggered by acid reflux
Loss of central pattern generator function
Relaxation of pharyngeal muscles during sleep, causing upper airway collapse
Bronchospasm in the lower airways
Correct! During sleep, pharyngeal muscles relax and can collapse the upper airway, especially when exacerbated by excess tissue weight around the neck (obesity). This is obstructive sleep apnea — distinct from central sleep apnea (e.g., Ondine's Curse).
Obstructive sleep apnea results from relaxation of the pharyngeal muscles during sleep, leading to upper airway collapse. Excess neck tissue weight (obesity) exacerbates this. It's distinct from central sleep apnea where the respiratory center itself fails. Review Slide 30.
Multiple Choice
A patient with untreated obstructive sleep apnea for several years develops elevated pulmonary artery pressures. What is the mechanism?
Left ventricular failure from systemic hypertension
Chronic intermittent hypoxia triggers hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction, leading to sustained pulmonary hypertension
Increased CO₂ directly constricts pulmonary vessels
Chronic snoring causes airway remodeling that increases pulmonary resistance
Correct! Each apneic episode produces intermittent hypoxia. Over months to years, repeated hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction becomes sustained, leading to pulmonary hypertension. This then increases right ventricular afterload, eventually causing right heart failure.
The mechanism is hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction. Each apneic episode causes intermittent hypoxia → pulmonary vasoconstriction. Over time, this becomes sustained → pulmonary hypertension → right ventricular hypertrophy → right heart failure. Review Slide 30.
Clinical Scenario
A 55-year-old obese male presents with daytime somnolence and loud snoring. Labs show hemoglobin 18.5 g/dL. Physical exam reveals JVD and peripheral edema. What is the most likely unifying diagnosis, and what explains the elevated hemoglobin?
This is obstructive sleep apnea with downstream consequences. The cascade: chronic intermittent hypoxia → (1) pulmonary hypertension → right heart failure (explaining JVD and edema), and (2) activation of the HIF-2α pathway in the kidneys → ↑ erythropoietin → secondary polycythemia (explaining the elevated hemoglobin). First-line treatment: CPAP — continuous positive airway pressure splints the airway open during sleep, breaks the cycle of intermittent hypoxia, and allows the downstream pathology to begin reversing.

Correct! Cheyne-Stokes occurs via two mechanisms: delayed transit time (heart failure, elevated ICP) and gain instability (high altitude, brainstem lesions, end of life). OSA is a separate entity — it involves mechanical upper airway obstruction, not a chemoreceptor feedback malfunction.
Cheyne-Stokes is associated with heart failure and elevated ICP (via delayed transit time) and high altitude, brainstem lesions, and end of life (via gain instability in the chemoreceptor feedback loop). OSA has a different mechanism — mechanical upper airway obstruction, not feedback malfunction. Review Slide 31.
Clinical Thinking
Why does heart failure predispose a patient to Cheyne-Stokes breathing? Think about what happens to cardiac output and circulation time.
In heart failure, decreased cardiac output means blood takes longer to travel from the lungs to the brain. This creates a delay in the chemoreceptor feedback loop — by the time the brain detects a change in blood gases, it overcorrects. This overcorrection then takes time to be sensed again, creating the characteristic waxing-and-waning breathing pattern of Cheyne-Stokes.
